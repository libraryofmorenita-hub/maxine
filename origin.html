The problem

A patient who could speak, but not press a button.

Dr. Mauricio Lizama works with quadriplegic patients in Puerto Rico — patients with limited or no use of their hands. Standard hospital beds are operated with wired push-button controllers. For many of Dr. Lizama's patients, those buttons are inaccessible.

VME (Volunteers for Medical Engineering) brought this challenge to Loyola's Engineering Design program. The ask: design a voice-controlled interface that would let patients reposition a hospital bed using spoken commands — without expensive commercial assistive tech.

Our team of four took on the project for EG 497/498 and delivered a working prototype with a trained vocabulary of seven commands, a trigger-word activation system, and a physical scale model of the bed mechanism.

System architecture

Two enclosures. One voice.

The system is split into two physical units connected by wiring through the bed frame. The patient-facing top box handles all audio input and user feedback. The bottom box, hidden in the base of the bed, contains the processing and relay hardware.

Top Box — Patient Unit

4″ × 4″ × 3″ enclosure.
LCD display · Microphone · Speaker · Joystick override.
Shows current command received and system status. Joystick provides a non-voice fallback for caregivers.

Bottom Box — Control Unit

10″ × 10″ × 5″ enclosure.
Arduino Mega · EasyVR 3 Plus (Fortebit) · Relay board.
Receives decoded command from VR chip, activates the correct relay to drive the actuator motor.

The EasyVR 3 Plus is a speaker-dependent voice recognition module — it is trained on a specific person's voice, which gives it a high rejection rate for strangers and background noise. All seven commands were trained by a member of the team and verified against the Harvard Sentence corpus to measure false-positive performance.

The bed mechanism is demonstrated with a 1:2 scale aluminum frame model driven by MaoPiner linear actuators, matching the geometry of a real clinical bed.

Voice interface

Seven commands. One trigger word.

The system uses a two-step activation pattern: the patient first says the trigger word to wake the chip from standby, then speaks a command. This prevents unintended actuation from ambient speech in a clinical environment.

How the name started

"Maxine" was chosen as the trigger word because it is phonetically distinct, uncommon in clinical speech, and easy to pronounce clearly. It was trained into the EasyVR chip. When we built the next project — a full patient monitoring system — it inherited the name. Maxine it is.

Performance

Tested. Measured. Documented.

All testing was conducted in a quiet lab environment and a simulated clinical environment with background noise (TV, conversation). Commands were tested 10 times per condition. Harvard sentences were used to measure false-positive (unintended actuation) performance.

The 100% Harvard sentence rejection rate is the most important safety metric: the system never activated a relay in response to normal clinical conversation during testing. The 90% trigger word recognition rate reflects the speaker-dependent nature of the EasyVR chip — recognition was near-perfect for the trained voice, with expected degradation under high ambient noise.

Budget

$600 given. $384.82 spent.

VME provided a $600 budget for the full prototype. The final bill of materials came to $384.82 — under budget by over $215. All components were commercially available, off-the-shelf parts, consistent with VME's goal of producing a replicable, low-cost solution.

What came next

One question opened a hundred more.

Completing the VME bed project made one clinical gap impossible to ignore: patients with limited mobility who need voice control of a bed almost certainly also need low-cost, accessible monitoring of their own vital signs — especially in home or resource-limited settings. No such open-source option existed.

The future vision documented in the pitch deck listed the next steps for the bed itself: vital signs integration, ambient temperature control, voice audio responses, and position memory presets. Each of those features pointed toward a standalone monitoring system.

That system became Maxine.

  • Vital signs tracking (HR, SpO₂, temperature, respiration) → became the Maxine sensor suite
  • Ambient temperature monitoring → became the MLX90614 IR sensor integration
  • Voice audio responses → carried forward as a future Maxine feature
  • Position memory presets → informed the BLE command protocol design
  • Low-cost, open, replicable → the entire design philosophy of Maxine

The name carried forward too. "Maxine" — the trigger word trained into the EasyVR chip — became the name of the project, the repository, and eventually this research platform.

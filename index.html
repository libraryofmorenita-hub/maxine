The origin

A patient in Puerto Rico. A question that wouldn't let go.

Maxine began inside a hospital bed — literally. Through Volunteers for Medical Engineering and Loyola's Engineering Design Project, our team built a voice-activated bed control system for Dr. Mauricio Lizama's quadriplegic patients in Puerto Rico. Patients who could not press a button. Patients who needed to speak to move.

That project answered one question and opened a hundred more. If we could restore voice control to a bed, what else could we restore? Maxine is the answer we're still building: an open, modular monitoring system that gives patients information about their own bodies — and gives caregivers the ability to act.

The name Maxine is also the trigger word the original VME device listens for. It felt right to carry that forward.

What Maxine is

Modular. Wireless. Honest about what it is.

Three principles shape every design decision Maxine makes.

Not a medical device. Not for clinical diagnosis. Maxine is open-source hardware and software for education, research, and personal exploration.
01

Restore autonomy through awareness

Patients and caregivers who understand what a body is doing can respond faster, advocate better, and feel less alone. Maxine puts that information where it belongs — at the bedside, in plain language.

Heart rate SpO2 Temperature Blood pressure Respiration
02

Democratize the tools of care

A commercial multi-parameter vital signs monitor costs thousands of dollars. The Maxine hardware bill of materials is under $120. Every design choice — ESP32, BLE, e-paper — was made to keep the barrier of entry low.

ESP32 MicroPython BLE wireless E-paper display <$120 BOM
03

Build a foundation the community can extend

Maxine is a scaffold, not a finished product. The sensor driver API is designed so new sensors slot in without touching the core. The BLE protocol is documented. The code is yours to fork, teach with, and improve.
